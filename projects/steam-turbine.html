Objective

The task was to assess the structural and dynamic condition of an in-use steam turbine blade and provide a well-evidenced recommendation as to whether it was suitable for recommissioning. The approach centred on experimental modal analysis using an instrumented roving hammer test.

🎓 Achieved a grade of 73% — 1st Class — for the analysis, reporting, and recommendations produced.

Experimental Method

A roving hammer test was performed on the turbine blade, where an instrumented impact hammer was used to excite the structure at multiple defined locations while measuring the dynamic response. This non-destructive testing technique allows the frequency response functions (FRFs) of the structure to be characterised without disassembly.

  • Collected 8 datasets across 10 measurement locations on the blade surface
  • Response signals were acquired and processed to obtain frequency response functions
  • Data collection followed a systematic roving pattern to ensure spatial coverage

Analysis & Results

All data processing and analysis was conducted in MATLAB. Curve fitting models were applied to the FRF data to extract key modal parameters, including natural frequencies, damping ratios, and mode shapes.

  • Extracted natural frequencies and damping ratios using rational fraction polynomial curve fitting
  • Constructed and visualised mode shapes from the extracted modal data
  • Computed Auto Modal Assurance Criterion (MAC) and Cross MAC matrices to evaluate mode shape consistency and orthogonality
  • Compared extracted frequencies against known operational excitation frequencies to assess risk of resonance

The MAC analysis confirmed good separation between the dominant mode shapes, providing confidence in the quality of the extracted modal parameters. The results were synthesised into a structured engineering recommendation regarding recommissioning suitability.
